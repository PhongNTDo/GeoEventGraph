# GeoKG Annotation Guidelines

These guidelines define how to create and review GeoKG gold annotations. The
goal is consistency: the gold data should represent what the article explicitly
supports, not what an annotator or model can infer from outside knowledge.

## Scope

Annotate geopolitical events from the article text. Keep the annotation focused
on events, actors, locations, relations, evidence, and provenance that are useful
for the GeoKG pipeline.

Do annotate:

- military attacks and strikes;
- threats, warnings, ultimatums, and stated intent;
- negotiations, talks, ceasefires, and diplomatic mediation;
- support, backing, assistance, or transfers;
- sanctions or proposed sanctions when explicitly reported;
- blockades, restrictions, or threats to restrict movement through a strategic
  route or location.

Do not annotate:

- broad background without a concrete event or claim;
- speculative analysis not attributed to an actor;
- purely domestic or personal details unless they are directly tied to a
  supported geopolitical event;
- duplicate events already represented by a clearer event in the same article.

## Evidence

Every event and relation must have an exact supporting quote from the article.

Use evidence that:

- is copied exactly from the article text;
- is short enough to identify the claim clearly;
- contains the core actor, action, and target/location when possible;
- supports the event without requiring outside knowledge.

Avoid evidence that:

- paraphrases the article;
- combines words from separate non-contiguous sentences;
- only supports context but not the actual event;
- is too broad when a more precise sentence exists.

If no exact quote supports the event, omit the event.

## Events

An event is one concrete geopolitical happening or explicitly reported claim.
Use one event per distinct action or claim.

Good event:

- "Israel attacked the South Pars gas field."
- "Iran warned it would close the Strait of Hormuz."
- "The United States and Iran held talks in Geneva."

Too broad:

- "The conflict escalated."
- "The region faced uncertainty."

Too inferred:

- "Country A supported Country B" when the article only says the two leaders met
  without any explicit support.

## Claims And Factuality

If the article reports a claim, annotate the event as the claim states it, but
make the summary and evidence clear that it is reported or attributed.

Example:

- Summary: "Israel said it attacked Iran's navy in the Caspian Sea."
- Evidence: exact sentence where the article attributes the claim.

Do not convert claimed events into confirmed facts unless the article itself
does so.

## Event Dates

Use the most specific event date explicitly available in the article.

Date precision:

- `day`: the event date is known as YYYY-MM-DD.
- `month`: only YYYY-MM is supported.
- `year`: only YYYY is supported.
- `article_date`: the event date is not stated, so use the article publication
  date.
- `unknown`: no reliable event date can be assigned.

Use `article_date` only when the event is presented as current or recent and no
better date is given.

Historical background should keep its historical date if the article states one.
Do not force old events to the article publication date.

## Locations And Manual Geocoding

Use the most specific concrete event location stated in the article.

Good locations:

- `Strait of Hormuz`
- `South Pars`
- `Ras Laffan industrial area`
- `Haifa`

Weak locations:

- `Middle East`
- `Gulf`
- `Iranian ports`
- `school`

Weak locations may still be annotated if they are the only location stated, but
they should be reviewed manually before map use.

LLMs may suggest coordinates, but coordinates are not gold until reviewed. Manual
coordinate corrections should be recorded in the geocode review CSV generated by
the postprocessing pipeline. The map should prefer reviewed `suggested_*`
coordinates when present, and fall back to `current_*` coordinates otherwise.

## Entities

Annotate only entities that are directly relevant to events, relations, or event
context.

Current entity types:

- `NationState`
- `NonStateActor`
- `PoliticalLeader`
- `StrategicLocation`
- `MilitaryAsset`

Use canonical English names where obvious:

- `United States`, not `US`
- `United Kingdom`, not `UK`
- `Strait of Hormuz`, not `Hormuz Strait`, unless the article only gives an
  ambiguous form and the canonical form is uncertain.

If the current ontology lacks a perfect type, choose the closest valid type and
add an annotation note if it affects interpretation. This is expected to improve
in ontology v2.

## Core Participants

Event participants should be the core actors, targets, places, and assets needed
to define the event. Do not add every named entity in the sentence as a
participant.

Core participants are different from context entities:

- Core participant: needed to understand what happened.
- Context entity: mentioned nearby but not structurally necessary.

For now, the schema has only `participants`, so keep that list conservative.
Prefer missing optional context over adding noisy participants.

## Required Roles By Event Type

### AttackEvent

Required when stated:

- `initiator`: actor carrying out or claiming the attack.
- `target`: actor, asset, facility, or location attacked.

Optional:

- `affected_location`: concrete place where the attack occurred.
- `military_asset`: weapon, aircraft, missile, ship, or other asset used.
- `participant`: actor materially involved but not cleanly initiator or target.

If the article states an attack happened but not who carried it out, omit
`initiator` rather than inventing one.

### ThreatEvent

Required when stated:

- `initiator`: actor issuing the threat or warning.
- `target`: actor, place, route, or asset being threatened.

Optional:

- `affected_location`: place or route that would be affected.
- `participant`: actor involved in the threat context.

### NegotiationEvent

Required:

- at least two `participant` roles, or one `initiator` plus one `target` if the
  article frames talks directionally.

Optional:

- `mediator`: actor mediating or hosting talks.
- `affected_location`: place where talks occur, if relevant.

Use `NEGOTIATED_WITH` relations between direct negotiating sides, not between a
mediator and every party unless the article states direct negotiation.

### SupportEvent

Required when stated:

- `supporter`: actor giving support.
- `target` or `participant`: actor receiving or benefiting from support.

Optional:

- `military_asset`: weapon, system, or equipment transferred or provided.
- `affected_location`: location where support is delivered or used.

Do not annotate support from vague alignment unless the article states support,
backing, aid, transfer, or assistance.

### SanctionEvent

Required:

- `sanctioning_actor`: actor imposing or proposing sanctions.
- `target`: actor, leader, organization, or state being sanctioned.

Optional:

- `participant`: actor materially involved in the sanctions context.

Do not annotate sanctions if the article only mentions general economic
pressure without a sanctioning actor or target.

### BlockadeEvent

Required when stated:

- `initiator`: actor imposing or threatening blockade/restriction.
- `target` or `affected_location`: route, port, country, or place affected.

Optional:

- `military_asset`: ships, fleets, mines, aircraft, or systems used to enforce
  the blockade.
- `participant`: actor involved in the blockade context.

Use `affected_location` for routes and waterways such as the Strait of Hormuz.

## Relations

Relations are compatibility graph edges derived from events. They should not
introduce facts not supported by the event evidence.

Allowed relation types:

- `ATTACKED`
- `THREATENED`
- `NEGOTIATED_WITH`
- `SUPPORTED`
- `SANCTIONED`
- `BLOCKADED`

Relation source and target should usually correspond to core participants.

Do not create a relation:

- between two actors that merely appear in the same sentence;
- when the direction is unclear;
- when the relation is only inferred from wider context;
- when no exact evidence quote supports it.

## Summaries

Event summaries should be one short, grounded sentence.

Good summary:

- "Iran warned it could close the Strait of Hormuz."

Bad summary:

- "Iran escalated tensions in the region."

If the event is a claim, keep attribution:

- "Netanyahu said Israel attacked Iran's navy in the Caspian Sea."

## Common Review Checks

Before setting `annotation_status` to `gold`, check:

- every event has exact evidence;
- every evidence quote appears in the article text;
- every event has the right type;
- event dates and date precision are correct;
- participant roles follow the event-type rules above;
- relation direction matches the article;
- location is the most specific stated event location;
- LLM-suggested coordinates have not been treated as reviewed truth.

## Known Current Weak Spots

The first evaluation run showed the strongest error patterns in:

- missing participants;
- participant role mismatches;
- event relation mismatches;
- exact evidence mismatch;
- missing or weak event coordinates.

When reviewing new annotations or changing extraction prompts, prioritize these
areas first.
